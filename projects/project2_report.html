Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

JoJo Lin — jol145@ucsd.edu

Dataset: Abortion Data by U.S. State from the Guttmacher Institute

Proposition

“States that reduced the number of abortion clinics between 2017 and 2020 also experienced declines in abortion rates — suggesting that clinic availability directly affects abortion access.”

Counterpoint takeaway: Clinic access declines don’t uniformly reduce abortion rates; state-level changes vary widely.

FOR the Proposition

When clinics close, abortion rates tend to fall.

Design Decisions, Scores (–2 to +2), and Rationale:

  • –1Similarity of hues for points and trendline. Using closely related colors visually fuses variables and strengthens perceived linkage without statistical proof.
  • –1Axis limits centered on the dense cluster. Cropping to the interquartile span reduces visible outliers and makes the slope appear more uniform.
  • –1Trendline shown without R² or confidence band. Viewers may overestimate the strength and certainty of the association.
  • 0State labels hidden by default. Reduces clutter for readability; exact state context is available on demand if shown in the appendix/figure notes.

AGAINST the Proposition

Clinic changes do not consistently predict state abortion-rate changes.

Design Decisions, Scores (–2 to +2), and Rationale:

  • +2Full, symmetric y-axis range (±100%). Prevents exaggeration and puts differences in appropriate context.
  • +1Distributional view (box plots) instead of single averages. Highlights overlap and variability, avoiding overstatement of effects.
  • 0Equal-sized bins for clinic change (<0, =0, >0). Simplifies a continuous predictor to aid comparison while acknowledging information loss.
  • +1Neutral palette and clear annotation of medians. Steers attention toward central tendency and spread rather than dramatic color cues.

Final Reflection

I find the opposing-side visualization more convincing. The “FOR” scatterplot is persuasive, but its color pairing, cropped axes, and unqualified trendline can imply a stronger relationship than warranted. The “AGAINST” box-plot view provides broader context—overlapping distributions and similar medians across clinic-change categories—illustrating that clinic availability alone does not consistently determine abortion-rate changes. This exercise reinforces how design choices (axes, encodings, and annotations) can shape perception without changing the underlying data; therefore, transparent labeling and conservative scaling are critical to honest analysis.
